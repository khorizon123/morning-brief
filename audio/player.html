World

Iran Buries Khamenei as New Supreme Leader Remains Out of Sight

Millions of mourners filled Tehran's streets for the funeral of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, killed in a U.S.-Israeli airstrike on February 28th that triggered the Iran war. His son and designated successor Mojtaba Khamenei has not been seen in public since assuming the role, raising questions about who is actually in charge. NYT journalists entered Iran for the first time since the war began and described the event as "part farewell, part spectacle and part turning point." Meanwhile, U.S. voters are souring on the conflict: an FT/Focaldata poll found 58% believe the war was not worth the cost, and 44% say it left the U.S. in a weaker position vis-à-vis Iran.

Iran's supreme leader is the country's paramount authority, above the elected president. Khamenei held the role for 35 years. The war with the U.S. and Israel ended in a ceasefire widely seen as favorable to Iran, in part because Tehran threatened to close the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20% of global oil flows — giving it critical leverage.

Russia Strikes Kyiv Again Ahead of NATO Summit in Ankara

Russia launched its second major attack on Kyiv in less than a week, killing at least seven people with drones and missiles. The assault comes as NATO leaders prepare to gather in Ankara, Turkey for the alliance's annual summit. Trump is expected to meet Zelensky there and is pushing to broker a ceasefire; the two leaders spoke by phone over the weekend, after which Zelensky said there was a "real prospect of ending" the war. Separately, Turkey detained two journalists and dozens of opposition figures ahead of the summit.

The NATO summit in Ankara is particularly fraught given Trump's repeated threats to pull the U.S. out of the alliance. Ukraine has been conducting a summer deep-strike drone campaign against Russian fuel infrastructure, aiming to pressure Moscow toward talks.

China Fires Ballistic Missile Into Pacific Nuclear-Free Zone

China launched a submarine-fired ballistic missile carrying a dummy warhead into the Pacific Ocean, its first such test since 2024. Beijing described it as a "routine part" of its annual military training, but the test drew condemnation from Japan, Australia, and New Zealand, who noted China's increasing projection of power in the region. The test lands amid elevated tensions in the Taiwan Strait, where China also sent two vessels to patrol waters east of the island.

The Pacific Ocean is designated a nuclear-free zone under the Treaty of Rarotonga. China's growing military posture — particularly around Taiwan and in the South China Sea — has been a central concern for U.S. allies in the Indo-Pacific.

Germany Plans €800bn in Borrowing for Historic Rearmament Drive

Germany announced plans to borrow more than €800bn by 2030, a dramatic break from the constitutional "debt brake" that has defined the country's fiscal conservatism for decades. Chancellor Friedrich Merz is directing the spending toward defense levels not seen since German reunification. The shift reflects Europe's urgent push to build its own military capacity as U.S. commitment to NATO becomes less certain under Trump.

Germany's debt brake was enshrined in its constitution in 2009, limiting net new borrowing to 0.35% of GDP annually. The country has historically been the most resistant of large European nations to deficit spending, making this a generational policy reversal.

Sudan Crisis Deepens as RSF Encircles City of Half a Million

The Rapid Support Forces (RSF) paramilitary has encircled el-Obeid, capital of Sudan's North Kordofan state, trapping an estimated 500,000 civilians, destroying fuel stations and bridges with drones. The U.S. State Department has warned that "mass atrocities could be imminent." The RSF's record is grim: when it overran el-Fasher in Darfur last October, it is estimated to have killed or raped tens of thousands of civilians.

Sudan has been in civil war since April 2023, when the national army and the RSF — formerly allied — turned on each other. The conflict has created one of the world's worst humanitarian crises and has largely been overshadowed by other global events, earning it the label "the forgotten war."

Markets & Deals

UK Media Consolidation: Sky Buys ITV's TV Business for £1.6bn

Sky has agreed to acquire ITV's broadcast television operations for £1.6bn, uniting Britain's two largest commercial broadcasters under the same roof. The buyer, Sky, is owned by Comcast, which has simultaneously announced plans to spin off Sky and NBCUniversal from its U.S. cable and broadband business — a significant restructuring of one of America's largest media conglomerates. The Sky-ITV combination will reach 21 million British households. Separately, Paramount Skydance is expected to close a $111bn acquisition of Warner Bros Discovery later this summer, and Fox Corporation agreed to buy streaming platform Roku for $22bn. The deals collectively reflect a defensive posture by traditional broadcasters bulking up against Netflix, YouTube, and social media.

EasyJet Agrees in Principle to £5.5bn Takeover by Castlelake

EasyJet, Britain's biggest budget airline, has accepted in principle a £5.5bn ($7.3bn) takeover bid from Castlelake, a U.S. private credit and alternative investment firm. Castlelake had previously made four rejected offers, the last valuing the airline at £4.9bn. The American firm has until August 3rd to submit a formal offer. EasyJet shares surged in London on the news. The deal continues a trend of private capital firms acquiring traditional European businesses under financial pressure.

Solstice Advanced Materials and Element Solutions in $14.5–27bn Chemicals Merger

Solstice Advanced Materials, a specialty chemicals company that spun out of Honeywell last year, is in advanced talks to merge with Element Solutions, a publicly traded firm that makes materials used by chipmakers. The combined entity would be valued at roughly $14.5bn to $27bn depending on structure, creating a market leader in advanced materials for electronics and automotive manufacturers. Element Solutions shares have surged in recent weeks ahead of the announcement. The deal adds to a busy M&A cycle in industrial materials as AI-driven chip demand raises the premium on specialty inputs.

OPEC+ Raises Output Quotas, Raising Glut Risk as Brent Sits Around $70

Seven OPEC+ members collectively increased August production quotas by 188,000 barrels per day, continuing a ramp-up of nearly 1 million barrels per day since April. The moves had been blocked from reaching markets during the Iran war by the closed Strait of Hormuz, but with the waterway now open and Brent crude just above $70, the cartel risks contributing to a global supply glut. Brent fell roughly 0.6% early Monday. Some analysts forecast sharp further price declines, which could ease inflation pressure on central banks — a notable secondary effect for rate watchers.

Meta's 'Watermelon' Model Said to Match GPT-5.5 While Still in Training

Meta's superintelligence chief Alexandr Wang told employees that the company's next frontier model, code-named Watermelon, has already matched OpenAI's GPT-5.5 in internal benchmarks — despite still being in training. Watermelon runs on roughly 10x the compute of Meta's current best model, Muse Spark. Wang also teased that an update to Muse Spark with "big coding and agentic gains" is imminent, and that an "Opus-level" coding model (referring to Anthropic's top-tier Claude Opus) is coming "pretty soon." CEO Zuckerberg tempered expectations at the same town hall by saying industry-wide agent progress "hasn't really accelerated" as expected — though Wang clarified Zuck was talking about the broader field, not Meta specifically.

Alibaba Bans Employees from Using Claude Code Amid China-User Security Concerns

Alibaba reportedly ordered staff to wipe Anthropic's Claude Code — an AI coding assistant — from work computers, after classifying it as high-risk. The ban follows Anthropic's efforts to prevent unauthorized access and model "distillation," a technique where a company trains its own AI by feeding it outputs from a competitor's model, essentially copying its capabilities. Employees were redirected to Alibaba's own Qoder tool. The move highlights the intensifying IP and national-security tensions around frontier AI tools in the China-U.S. context.

Trump Administration Warns ASML Machine May Have Reached China

The Trump administration has thrown Dutch semiconductor equipment maker ASML into a diplomatic and legal crisis by warning that one of its extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines — the world's most advanced chip-making tool, and made exclusively by ASML — may have reached China. ASML flatly denies it. EUV machines are the crucial chokepoint in global semiconductor manufacturing; without them, it is currently impossible to produce the most advanced chips. The U.S. has been pushing the Netherlands to restrict ASML exports to China since 2022, making this escalation potentially consequential for global chip supply chains.

Company

SoftBank / Masayoshi Son

SoftBank, the Japanese tech investment conglomerate founded 45 years ago by Masayoshi Son, recently overtook Toyota to become Japan's most valuable company for the first time since the dot-com bubble — a remarkable comeback for a man whose career has swung between visionary genius and spectacular collapse. Son has bet the house on AI, pouring billions into data centers, AI models, chips, and robotics; SoftBank's Vision Fund was already the world's largest tech investment vehicle, and Son is now positioning himself as an indispensable broker between U.S. and Middle Eastern capital and the global AI buildout. The surprising fact: SoftBank's 2000-era peak valuation collapsed by 99% during the dot-com bust, wiping out roughly $70 billion of Son's paper wealth in a single year — yet he rebuilt it entirely.
