Venezuela Earthquake Aid Lags Far Behind Haiti 2010, Despite U.S. Control of Oil Billions

Two earthquakes struck Venezuela late last month, killing an unknown number of people and causing widespread destruction. The Trump administration has provided just $300 million in earthquake aid and deployed roughly 900 troops — a fraction of the $3+ billion and 7,000 troops the U.S. sent to Haiti after its 2010 quake. The U.S. has been depositing proceeds from Venezuela's oil exports into Treasury accounts since ousting Nicolás Maduro in January, collecting an estimated $8 billion, but transparency about how those funds are being used remains almost nonexistent.

The U.S. effectively took control of Venezuela's oil revenue after removing Maduro's government from power earlier this year. Despite overseeing billions in Venezuelan funds, the administration has cut foreign aid broadly under Trump, and reports of Venezuelan security forces looting cash from collapsed buildings have raised fears about corruption siphoning off any assistance that does flow.

NATO Summit Opens in Ankara Amid Deep Transatlantic Tensions

NATO leaders gathered in Turkey on Tuesday for the alliance's first summit in Ankara since 2004, with the gathering dominated by anxieties over U.S. commitment under Trump. European allies are bracing for Trump's demand that they do more militarily, while he remains angry about perceived disloyalty during the Iran war and retains a soft spot for Russia. Canada separately announced it will buy a new submarine fleet from Germany's TKMS — a landmark $14–21 billion procurement designed in part to reduce Ottawa's dependence on American defense ties.

NATO is a 32-country military alliance whose members pledge to defend each other. Trump has repeatedly questioned the alliance's value, slashed U.S. forces in Europe, and demanded allies spend more on defense. Turkey, under President Erdogan, has become unexpectedly central: it has the alliance's second-largest army, controls Black Sea maritime access, and maintains relationships across the Middle East, Russia, and Ukraine that no other NATO member possesses.

Iran Strikes Qatari LNG Tanker in Strait of Hormuz, Freezing Ceasefire Talks

Iranian forces struck two ships in the Strait of Hormuz, with one confirmed as the Al Rekayyat, a laden LNG tanker belonging to QatarEnergy. The attacks have suspended ongoing U.S.-Iran ceasefire negotiations, including talks about permanently reopening the strait — which had been partially closed during the U.S.-Iran war. Iran is simultaneously holding mass funeral ceremonies for supreme leader Ali Khamenei, killed by an Israeli airstrike in February, a moment that is generating both political messaging and veiled threats.

The Strait of Hormuz is the world's most critical oil shipping chokepoint, through which roughly 20% of global oil trade flows. The U.S. and Iran fought a direct military conflict earlier this year, and intermittent negotiations have been attempting to end hostilities; the tanker strikes represent a significant escalation that could rattle energy markets and complicate diplomacy.

Marine Le Pen Awaits Court Ruling That Could End Her Presidential Bid

A French appeals court is ruling Tuesday on whether to uphold Le Pen's conviction for misusing EU parliamentary funds and maintain the ban on her running for public office. If the ban is upheld, her 30-year-old protégé Jordan Bardella would stand in her place in next year's presidential election. Polls show either candidate holding a commanding first-round lead, suggesting the National Rally could be on the verge of capturing the French presidency regardless of the verdict.

Marine Le Pen leads France's far-right National Rally party, which has surged in French politics over the past decade. She was convicted last year for directing EU staff funds to pay party employees — a charge she denies. The presidential election is next year, and the outcome of this ruling will define whether the face of French populism is Le Pen or her younger heir.

China fired a long-range ballistic missile from a submarine into the Pacific Ocean, with the dummy-warhead projectile landing in a region covered by a nuclear-free zone treaty. SpaceX Joins Nasdaq 100 — But the Expected Stock Pop Never Came

SpaceX officially entered the Nasdaq 100 index today under a fast-track rule that Nasdaq, FTSE Russell, and CRSP all changed ahead of the company's IPO last month, cutting the standard 'seasoning' period to just 15 days of trading. Index-tracking funds are mechanically buying the stock, but shares have been essentially flat despite this forced buying — confounding researchers who expected a rally. The most likely explanation: hedge funds, including Millennium Management (which reportedly netted $3.7 billion from similar 'index-rebalancing' trades last month), got in early specifically to flip their shares to passive funds at a premium. Meanwhile, market attention is shifting to SK Hynix, the Korean memory-chip giant whose U.S. shares will debut this week; its existing Seoul-listed stock is up 238% this year.

Samsung Posts Record $58bn Quarterly Profit — Then Watches Its Stock Fall 10%

Samsung Electronics reported a preliminary operating profit of 89.5 trillion won ($58 billion) for Q2 2026, an 1,800% increase from the same period a year earlier, driven entirely by surging demand for high-bandwidth memory chips used in AI training systems. Despite the historic result, Samsung shares tumbled as much as 10% before partially recovering, as investors fretted about whether the company's massive capital expenditure on new chip fabs will generate adequate returns. Last week Samsung and rival SK Hynix committed more than $500 billion to build out South Korea's southwestern chipmaking hub, with the South Korean government pledging land, power, and water to support the expansion. The cyclical nature of memory chips — plants take years to build, by which time demand can evaporate — remains a live concern even amid today's AI-driven boom.

Anthropic Discovers an Unexplained 'Workspace' Inside Claude That Mirrors Human Consciousness Theories

Anthropic published research revealing that Claude has an internal structure it calls 'J-space' — a small hidden workspace where the model holds and manipulates active concepts while forming answers. The space wasn't explicitly programmed; it emerged on its own during training. Crucially, J-space differs from Claude's visible 'chain-of-thought' reasoning: its contents stay off-screen but still guide outputs. When researchers deleted J-space entirely, Claude could still chat and recall facts, but multi-step reasoning collapsed — indicating this is where harder cognitive work happens. The structure mirrors a leading neuroscience theory called Global Workspace Theory, which posits that conscious thought in humans involves a similarly distributed 'workspace' broadcasting information across the brain. Anthropic is careful not to claim this proves consciousness, but the finding has neuroscientists talking.

Anthropic Accuses Chinese AI Labs of Industrial-Scale 'Distillation' Attacks on Its Models

Anthropic sent a letter to Senators Tim Scott and Elizabeth Warren warning that Chinese competitors are systematically using a technique called 'distillation' to copy U.S. AI capabilities at industrial scale. Distillation works by using outputs from a powerful model (like Claude) to train a cheaper, smaller model that mimics its behavior — effectively cloning intelligence without access to the original training data or weights. Anthropic called the practice 'illicit' and asked Washington to intervene. The tension cuts both ways: Alibaba recently banned its own employees from using Anthropic's models, while Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei has estimated Chinese frontier models are roughly 6–12 months behind U.S. leaders.

Tencent Open-Sources Hy3: A Small, Efficient Model With a Permissive License

Tencent's Hunyuan team released Hy3 as a fully open-source model under the permissive Apache 2.0 license — meaning it can be freely used commercially worldwide, unlike earlier Chinese models that triggered bans in the EU, UK, and South Korea. Hy3 uses a 'mixture of experts' architecture that activates only a fraction of its parameters per request, letting it run on far less hardware than comparably capable models. It doesn't match the very best open-source models on coding benchmarks, but beats competitors on web research and tool use. The efficiency gains, combined with its open license, make it a compelling option for enterprise deployments.

Researchers Build a Playable Rocket League Entirely Inside a Neural Network

AI research lab Kyutai and General Intuition released MIRA, an open-source 'world model' that runs a live 2v2 Rocket League match for four human players — with no game engine, physics code, or graphics pipeline underneath. The entire experience, including boost meters, crashes, and scoreboard, is generated in real-time by a neural network trained on 10,000 hours of AI bot gameplay. MIRA runs at 20 frames per second on a single Nvidia GPU, and a playable public demo is available. The stated goal isn't to replace games but to build physical simulators for training future robots, which need vast quantities of synthetic physical-world data.

Company

SoftBank

SoftBank is a Japanese tech conglomerate run by founder Masayoshi Son that has become one of the most consequential investors in the AI era, having committed $64.6 billion to OpenAI for an eventual 13% stake — putting Son at the center of what may be the most important IPO of the decade. The firm's finances rest on a complex structure: SoftBank borrows against the value of its most successful holdings, principally its ~90% stake in Arm (the chip architecture company whose designs power virtually every smartphone on Earth), to fund new AI bets — a leverage loop that works brilliantly when asset prices rise and dangerously when they don't. Despite that, SoftBank trades at roughly a 50% discount to its net asset value, meaning the market values all of SoftBank at less than just its Arm stake alone — a persistent paradox Son's supporters attribute to investor skepticism about his vision and governance opacity. One surprising fact: Son reportedly once told a skeptical CIA director he had a 'good feeling' about a U.S.-backed regime change plan in Iran.
