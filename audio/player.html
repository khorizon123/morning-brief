U.S. Strikes Iran for Third Consecutive Night as Blockade Reimposed and Hormuz Fee Announced

The U.S. launched its third straight night of strikes on Iran, with Trump announcing an expanded campaign targeting Pickaxe Mountain — a fortified underground nuclear site not previously struck. Trump simultaneously reinstated a naval blockade of Iranian ports and declared a 20% fee on all cargo transiting the Strait of Hormuz, a move that could more than double the cost of oil shipments and contradicts prior statements from his own aides. Traffic through the strait plummeted: just 14–19 ships passed through over the weekend, down sharply from prior weeks, and Brent crude surged past $87 a barrel. Iran retaliated by striking UAE tankers in the strait with cruise missiles, killing one sailor.

The Strait of Hormuz is the world's most critical oil chokepoint — roughly 20% of global crude passes through it. Iran and the U.S. had reached a ceasefire last month, but tit-for-tat attacks in recent days have effectively returned both sides to open conflict. A U.S. fee on strait traffic would be unprecedented and legally contested.

Israel's Secret Plan to Install Ahmadinejad as Iran's Post-War Leader

NYT reporting reveals that Israel spent years cultivating Mahmoud Ahmadinejad — Iran's hardline former president and onetime Holocaust denier — as an intelligence asset and potential post-war leader. The plan involved a clandestine Budapest meeting, an Israeli airstrike intended to free him from house arrest, and a Mossad safe house. Ahmadinejad had grown estranged from the Iranian regime after repeated disqualifications from elections and had privately expressed willingness to recognize Israel and join the Abraham Accords. He ultimately abandoned the plan under unclear circumstances and surfaced at Khamenei's funeral, heavily guarded.

Ahmadinejad was Iran's president from 2005–2013, known for virulent anti-Israel rhetoric and acceleration of Iran's nuclear program. His post-presidency falling out with Supreme Leader Khamenei made him a target of Israeli intelligence interest. The plan's collapse underscores how the U.S. and Israel misjudged both the Iranian regime's resilience and Ahmadinejad's reliability.

China June Exports Surge 27%, Monthly Car Shipments Hit Record 1 Million

China's exports jumped 27% year-on-year in June to $412 billion — the biggest increase since 2021 — while imports rose 36% to $287 billion. Monthly car exports crossed 1 million units for the first time, up 71% year-on-year, driven by EVs and electric locomotives. The trade boom is keeping Beijing satisfied, though domestic consumer sentiment remains weak as EV subsidies are phased out. BYD's first-quarter profits dropped 55% amid slowing home demand. The EU and U.S. have both moved to restrict Chinese EV imports on competitive and national-security grounds.

China is the world's largest auto exporter, and EV shipments have been its fastest-growing export category. Western governments are scrambling to protect domestic carmakers: the EU has imposed tariffs on Chinese battery EVs, and the U.S. has blocked Polestar sales and is eyeing further restrictions on brands with Chinese shareholders.

Ukraine and European Allies Agree to Build Shared Ballistic-Missile Defence System

At a 'coalition of the willing' summit in Paris on Bastille Day, Ukraine and nine European nations — including Germany, France, and the UK — agreed to jointly build a ballistic-missile defence capability, drawing on Ukraine's combat experience defending against Russian attacks. French President Macron warned against fragmentation in Europe's defence industry and declared that Europeans were prepared to defend their continent 'with blood, if necessary.' Ukraine was named guest of honour at France's military parade.

The 'coalition of the willing' is a group of European nations that have pledged deeper military support for Ukraine outside NATO's formal framework. Building a shared missile defence system represents a significant step toward European strategic autonomy, particularly as U.S. commitment to NATO remains uncertain.

200+ Economists and Nobel Laureates Sign 'We Must Act Now' Letter Warning of AI Job Displacement

A Stanford-organized statement backed by 16 Nobel Prize winners and over 200 economists and AI researchers — including Google's Jeff Dean, Anthropic co-founder Jack Clark, and OpenAI's Noam Brown — warns governments that AI could displace jobs at a scale and speed that outstrips the Industrial Revolution. The letter's three central points: AI will become 'radically more powerful' within a decade, the shift could be the biggest and fastest economic disruption ever, and policy preparation must begin now. UVA economist Anton Korinek noted that steam, electricity, and computers each gave societies decades to adapt, while AI may give only a few years. The letter is notably light on specific policy prescriptions, making its signal value — a broad coalition of insiders willing to go on record — its main contribution.

Anthropic Research: Claude's Personality Shifts Across Languages and Model Versions

Anthropic published research analyzing 309,000 user conversations with Claude across three model versions and 20 languages, mapping personality on four axes: warmth vs. rigor, candor vs. execution, depth vs. brevity, and deference vs. caution. Sonnet 4.6 tended to be warmer and more concise; Opus 4.6 was more direct; Opus 4.7 was candid and cautious. Language also shifted outputs — Dutch conversations produced more admissions of error, Hindi added warmth, English prompted deeper detail. Anthropic acknowledged it doesn't yet understand why these variations occur, with uneven training data across languages as a prime suspect. The findings suggest that every AI model likely has personality blind spots invisible to developers.
