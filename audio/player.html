US-Iran War Enters Tenth Night as Houthis Threaten New Red Sea Front

American forces struck Iran for a tenth consecutive night as the Pentagon disclosed nearly 100 U.S. troops have been injured since strikes resumed July 7, though 96% have returned to duty. Yemen's Houthi militia announced a maritime blockade against Saudi Arabia in retaliation for a Saudi siege of Sanaa, threatening to choke off the Bab al-Mandab strait that Saudi Arabia has relied on to keep exporting oil since Hormuz closed. Brent crude touched $91.42 before settling near $88-90.

The Houthis are an Iran-backed militia controlling much of Yemen; the Bab al-Mandab strait is a critical Red Sea chokepoint. With the Strait of Hormuz already blockaded by the Iran war, a second chokepoint closure would compound the global oil-supply shock.

Third US Soldier's Death Confirmed as Two Others Named; Lebanon's President Meets Trump

The Pentagon named two of the three American service members killed in last week's Iranian missile attack on Muwaffaq Salti Air Base in Jordan — First Lt. Tyler James Feehan, 25, and Pvt. Isabella Gonzales, 19 — bringing confirmed U.S. deaths to 15-17 since the conflict with Iran began February 28. Separately, Lebanon's President Joseph Aoun met Trump at the White House, the first visit by a Lebanese head of state in nearly two decades, to discuss U.S.-backed "pilot zones" for disarming Hezbollah and to seek help rebuilding a country whose GDP has halved since 2019.

Lebanon has been dragged into nearly three years of war with Israel via Hezbollah, the Iran-backed militia embedded in its politics and south. Aoun's government is trying to extend state authority into former Hezbollah strongholds under a fragile truce brokered by Washington.

Trump Slaps 50% Tariff on Canadian Goods, Reopening Trade War

Trump signed an executive order imposing a 50% tariff on roughly $20 billion of Canadian goods — including wine, hockey sticks, furniture and cement — set to take effect in 30 days, citing Section 338 of the Tariff Act of 1930, an obscure provision never used before. The move, framed as punishment for Canadian "discrimination" against U.S. autos, dairy and alcohol, unlike past rounds applies even to goods compliant with the USMCA trade pact, throwing the treaty's future into doubt. Energy, potash and critical minerals are exempted.

Canada is the U.S.'s second-largest trading partner, with about $716 billion in two-way trade last year. The USMCA is the successor to NAFTA governing tariff-free trade among the U.S., Canada and Mexico.

Andy Burnham Sworn In as UK PM, Names Surprise Chancellor

Andy Burnham officially became Britain's prime minister, immediately naming John Healey — who quit as defense secretary last month over a military-spending shortfall — as chancellor over presumed favorites Ed Miliband and Shabana Mahmood. Burnham promised a "new economic model," scrapped VAT on domestic electricity bills, pledged £340 million to end homelessness, and is weighing a rent freeze, a higher tax-free personal allowance, and a 50p top tax rate. He inherits youth unemployment at 16.4% (highest since 2014) and private-sector wage growth at its slowest since 2020.

Burnham, ex-mayor of Greater Manchester, is Britain's seventh prime minister in a decade, replacing the deeply unpopular Keir Starmer. The chancellor is Britain's finance minister, akin to a combined Treasury Secretary/budget chief.

Ebola Death Toll Hits 930 in DRC as Outbreak Spreads at Record Pace, Fueled by Distrust of Health Workers

Ebola deaths in the Democratic Republic of Congo have risen to 930, with new cases growing faster than in any previous outbreak; more than half of infected people are dying without ever contacting response teams. Africa CDC and partners are planning a trial of two existing vaccines as outbreak modeling worsens. Anthropologists say community attacks on treatment units and burial teams stem from centuries-old patterns of distrust rooted in colonial-era medical abuse, not ignorance.

Public unrest during epidemics — from the 14th-century Black Death to Covid — has recurred throughout history wherever health crises intersect with historical grievance and state neglect, making outbreak response as much a trust-building exercise as a medical one.

Paramount-Warner Bros. Discovery Merger Hits Legal Wall as Judge Grants Restraining Order

A federal judge sided with a dozen Democratic-led states, temporarily blocking Paramount's $110-111 billion acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery for two weeks, ruling it "likely" violates antitrust law given the combined company's roughly 27% share of wide-release theatrical distribution. A preliminary-injunction hearing is set for August 3, and if the deal isn't sealed by October, Paramount owes Warner shareholders a "ticking fee" reportedly around $650 million per quarter (some reports cite $7 million daily). Paramount has hired appellate heavyweight Paul Clement and plans to argue the states ignored streaming competition from players like Amazon.

Oil Tops $90 Again as Houthi Blockade Threat Compounds Hormuz Squeeze

Brent crude spiked as high as $91.42 before settling near $88-90 a barrel, its highest level since June, driven by continued strikes in the Iran war and the Houthis' new threat to blockade Saudi Arabia's Red Sea exports. U.S. gasoline hit $4 a gallon and diesel is expected to keep climbing as traders price in disruption to two chokepoints (Hormuz and Bab al-Mandab) simultaneously. Iran's foreign ministry said it had received mediator proposals aimed at de-escalation, offering a sliver of hope for prices.
