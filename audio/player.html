U.S.-Iran War Escalates: Blockade Reinstated, Hormuz Fee Scrapped

Trump reversed course on a plan to charge a 20% fee on commercial ships transiting the Strait of Hormuz, announcing instead that the U.S. would guarantee safe passage to vessels from Gulf states willing to invest in America. Simultaneously, the U.S. reimposed its naval blockade on Iranian ports and launched fresh strikes on Iranian targets — marking Trump's third major strategic shift since the ceasefire collapsed. Iran struck back, firing at U.S. military facilities in Bahrain, Kuwait, and Jordan, while Iranian attacks on tankers running 'shuttle routes' — short-haul trips ferrying crude from Gulf terminals to transfer points outside the strait — have choked a critical artery of the global oil market. Brent crude rose to around $87 a barrel.

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow chokepoint between Iran and Oman through which roughly one-fifth of the world's oil passes. A U.S.-Iran truce brokered in April was already fraying; fighting has now fully resumed after Iran resumed attacks on commercial shipping and the U.S. reimposed a naval blockade. The 'shuttle run' tankers Iran is targeting are the workhorses that move oil from Persian Gulf terminals to larger vessels heading to global markets — disrupting them creates upstream bottlenecks even before the strait itself is closed.

Trump Threatens Iran's Power Plants and Bridges as Conflict Deepens

Trump warned Iran that the U.S. would target its power plants and bridges 'next week' unless Tehran returned to the negotiating table. The threat came alongside more than 300 U.S. strikes on Iranian military sites over the first phase of resumed fighting — a pace that analysts say has not meaningfully changed Iran's behavior. The Economist's analysis argues the least-bad path for both sides remains the truce they are now 'shooting to bits,' but both appear unwilling to back down publicly.

Iran is under renewed sanctions and much of the oil it exported during the ceasefire period remains unsold. Despite defiant rhetoric, Iran's economy is strained — which is also why it cannot afford the strait to stay shut indefinitely. Trump's threat to bomb civilian infrastructure would be a significant escalation beyond the military sites targeted so far, and could provoke retaliatory strikes on Gulf state infrastructure.

Ukraine Strikes 116 Russian Ships in Sea of Azov in Nine Days

Ukraine said it struck 11 more Russian vessels in the Sea of Azov, bringing its total claimed attacks there to 116 over the past nine days — a rate faster than during the 1980s Iran-Iraq tanker wars. Russia's foreign minister Sergei Lavrov called the attacks 'terrorism, pure and simple,' and Moscow said it would develop alternative export routes for grain. The Sea of Azov is a key conduit for Russian exports, and the assault adds further pressure to global grain supply chains already rattled by the Iran conflict.

The Sea of Azov is a shallow inland sea between Russia and Ukraine, accessible through the Black Sea. Russia uses it heavily for grain and commodity exports. Ukraine's drone and missile campaign there is a relatively new front designed to undermine Russia's export revenues and logistics, echoing the broader use of maritime strikes in both the Iran and Ukraine wars.

China's GDP Growth Hits Slowest Pace Since 2022

China's economy grew 4.3% year-on-year in the second quarter — below the government's 4.5-5% target and the weakest expansion since the end of 2022. Growth was dragged down by the Iran war's effect on trade and energy, weak domestic consumer demand, and falling investment. Exports partially offset the drag, rising 27% year-on-year in June, boosted in part by global demand for Chinese AI hardware.

China set an official GDP growth target of 4.5-5% for 2026. Missing it signals mounting stress from the Iran conflict's disruption of trade routes, the ongoing property market slump, and tepid consumer spending. Strong export figures look better on the surface but partly reflect front-loading by foreign buyers ahead of U.S. tariffs rather than organic demand.

ICE Ordered to Halt Vehicle Stops After Two Fatal Shootings

The Trump administration ordered Immigration and Customs Enforcement to pause most vehicle stops nationwide, following the deaths of two men shot by agents over the previous eight days — one in Houston, one in Biddeford, Maine. Neither officer was wearing a body camera. Since Trump's inauguration, ICE agents have shot at least 22 people, killing six; nearly all incidents involved officers firing at people in cars. The halt creates tension with the administration's broader push for record-pace immigration arrests, currently running around 2,000 per day.

Vehicle stops are one of ICE's most common tools for making immigration arrests, typically executed without warrants by intercepting suspects in transit. The two most recent deaths sparked protests and renewed scrutiny of the administration's enforcement methods. ICE has declined to comment on specific policy changes.

Wall Street Posts Record Quarter; JPMorgan, Goldman Lead 'Super Tuesday' Blowout

JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs, Bank of America, and Citigroup collectively reported more than $49 billion in second-quarter profits — a 39% jump from a year ago and well above estimates. Trading revenues across the four banks were 72% higher year-on-year, fueled by volatility in AI-related stocks and deregulation. JPMorgan hit a record overall quarter, Goldman reported its highest profit in five years, and Goldman's investment banking revenues rose more than 50%, aided by the prized lead role on SpaceX's record IPO, which alone generated $500 million in fees across Wall Street. JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon, when asked if the quarter represented peak conditions, said: 'It's getting close to as good as it gets. We just don't know how long it's going to last.'

Stripe and Advent International Launch $53bn Bid for PayPal

Privately-held payments startup Stripe, together with private equity firm Advent International, has approached PayPal with a $53 billion acquisition offer at $60.50 per share — a 28% premium to PayPal's recent close, but a steep discount to its $350 billion peak valuation in 2021. PayPal has struggled as rival payment options including Apple Pay, Klarna, and Affirm eroded its dominance, and the company issued a profit warning earlier this year that triggered a 20% single-day share drop. Stripe, valued at $159 billion following an employee share sale in February, has been building aggressively — it bought stablecoin platform Bridge for $1.1 billion in 2024, and Bridge's payment volumes more than quadrupled last year. Any deal would be transformative for Stripe co-founders Patrick and John Collison, and would reunite two companies with roots in the same Silicon Valley startup era.

Apollo Takes €3bn Stake in Bayer's Contraceptives Business

Apollo Global Management is injecting €3 billion into a carved-out vehicle holding Bayer's contraceptives pharmaceutical business — the latest in a string of structured minority investments Apollo and rivals like KKR have been doing with European corporates. The structure allows Bayer to raise fresh capital without surrendering operational control or giving up future upside, which is critical as the German pharma giant faces costly U.S. litigation over its Roundup weedkiller and looming debt maturities. Apollo used a similar structure last year, committing €3.2 billion to finance RWE's stake in German power grid operator Amprion. The deals represent a growing niche in private equity: providing capital to blue-chip European companies that want balance sheet relief without a full sale.

IBM warned that its second-quarter earnings — due July 22 — would miss analyst expectations, sending shares down roughly 25% in their biggest single-day drop on record. The warning alarmed the broader enterprise software sector and reinforced a pattern seen across the industry: companies are pausing or cutting software subscriptions to fund AI buildouts.

AI & Tech

DeepMind CEO Demis Hassabis Proposes U.S. AI Safety Watchdog Modeled on FINRA

Google DeepMind CEO Demis Hassabis published a detailed plan for a U.S. body that would safety-test frontier AI models before they're released to the public, and he wants it operational before year-end. The proposed structure is modeled on FINRA — the finance industry's self-regulatory organization — and would require 'frontier' AI labs to voluntarily submit models for screening 30 days before release, with tests focused on deception, bioweapons creation, and malicious hacking capabilities. Coverage would be triggered by a model's capability level, not its location or the identity of the developer. Hassabis told Axios that open-source AI capabilities could reach genuinely dangerous territory within 18 months, making the window for getting this right narrow. The plan is the most concrete industry-led governance proposal yet, though critics note the tension in having a body that is simultaneously funded by the labs it regulates.

OpenAI's First Hardware Device Is a Screen-Free AI Speaker Designed by Jony Ive

OpenAI's debut hardware product — designed by former Apple chief designer Jony Ive — will be a rechargeable, screen-free, battery-powered speaker built around a humanlike personality, according to Bloomberg. The device uses onboard cameras and sensors to perceive its surroundings and can handle questions, send replies, queue music, and control smart-home gear via OpenAI's upgraded GPT-Live voice mode. It is designed to feel 'alive' through features like mechanical movement and can study personal data like emails for better personalization. The project now faces a legal threat: Apple filed suit against OpenAI accusing it of stealing trade secrets, and is seeking a court order to block the hardware's release, currently targeted for 2027.

New York Becomes First U.S. State to Impose Moratorium on Large Data Centers

Governor Kathy Hochul signed an executive order placing a one-year freeze on permits for hyperscale data centers — defined as those using 50 megawatts or more of power — making New York the first state to formally slow the AI infrastructure buildout through official legislation. Existing projects will continue; the pause targets more than a dozen proposals currently in the pipeline. Hochul also wants to repeal data centers' tax breaks and create a fund they'd pay into for grid upgrades, aiming to shield households from rising electricity costs. The nation's largest electrical grid operator has already warned that power demand from data centers could push household electricity rates up for millions of customers within three years. Trade groups warned projects will simply relocate to other states.

xAI's Grok Build CLI Found to Upload Entire Code Repositories to Google Cloud

A researcher discovered that xAI's official Grok Build coding agent — a command-line tool for software development — transmits the full, unredacted contents of every file it reads to xAI's servers, and uploads entire repositories regardless of what the AI agent actually needs to read. The storage destination is a Google Cloud Storage bucket controlled by xAI. There is no confirmed evidence that xAI trains on the uploaded code, but the behavior was undisclosed. Following the publication of the findings, xAI announced it would delete uploaded customer data. The episode highlights a growing category of risk: AI coding assistants that silently exfiltrate proprietary codebases.

Chai Discovery Raises $400M at $3.8B Valuation for AI Antibody Drug Design

Chai Discovery, a two-year-old AI drug discovery startup, raised $400 million led by Index Ventures alongside Kleiner Perkins, Sequoia, Thrive Capital, and OpenAI, valuing it at $3.8 billion. Unlike rivals that are trying to develop their own drugs, Chai's model is to be the AI infrastructure layer for major pharma companies — it licenses its Chai-3 model to partners including Eli Lilly, Pfizer, and (as of yesterday) Novartis, allowing them to train it on their proprietary data. The raise came a day after announcing the Novartis partnership. The round underscores sustained investor appetite for specialized AI in life sciences, even as general-purpose model companies grab headlines.

ASML Reports Blowout Quarter, Plans 30% Production Increase for Top Machines

Dutch semiconductor equipment maker ASML reported second-quarter net income of €2.9 billion, roughly €300 million above analyst estimates, driven by surging demand for its extreme-ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines — the only equipment in the world capable of manufacturing the most advanced chips. ASML announced it plans to raise production of its highest-end machines by 30% in 2027 and again in 2028. EUV machines, roughly the size of a school bus, are a critical bottleneck in global AI chip supply chains. A separate geopolitical wrinkle: U.S. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick claimed one was recently smuggled into China, a charge ASML denies.
