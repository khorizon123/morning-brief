U.S.-Iran Hostilities Escalate as Hormuz Standoff Deepens

The U.S. and Iran exchanged strikes for a second consecutive day, with American forces hitting railway bridges near Mashhad — the city where Supreme Leader Khamenei was buried — and Iran targeting U.S. bases in the region. Strait of Hormuz crossings dropped sharply as the ceasefire effectively collapsed, with maritime data firm Windward declaring the strait is 'once again operating under full conflict conditions.' Oil prices had briefly retreated to pre-war levels during a pause in fighting, but crude stockpiles remain critically low — the Cushing, Oklahoma hub has reached limits that make further withdrawals difficult, and U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve levels sit at their lowest since 1983. Separately, Israel warned Washington of a fresh Iranian plot to assassinate President Trump.

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman through which roughly 20% of global oil supply passes. Iran has long viewed the ability to threaten or close the strait as its primary geopolitical leverage. The current dispute centers on a clause in the U.S.-Iran memorandum of understanding that the two sides interpret differently — Iran's hard-liners believe it grants them administrative control over the waterway, while the U.S. sees it as simply requiring safe passage.

Iran's Hard-Liners Gain Ground as Internal Power Struggle Intensifies

Hard-line factions used Khamenei's funeral to physically attack officials who had defended the ceasefire — crowds tried to tackle President Pezeshkian and pelted Foreign Minister Araghchi with rocks. Shortly after these attacks, the Revolutionary Guards fired on three tankers in the strait. The new supreme leader, Mojtaba Khamenei, has yet to appear publicly after being wounded in strikes that killed his father, wife, and daughter, and his absence has allowed hard-liners to claim he is being manipulated by those pushing for peace.

Iran's political system involves factions that compete for influence around the supreme leader's office. The hard-liners' case rests on a belief that the U.S. never genuinely wanted peace — only a pause before the next war — and that negotiating from a position of weakness will only invite more pressure. The moderate faction around Pezeshkian argues that diplomacy is the only path to lifting crippling economic sanctions.

Bipartisan U.S. Housing Bill Set to Become Law Without Trump's Signature

A landmark bipartisan housing bill — the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act — is poised to become law automatically at midnight after a 10-day clock started when Speaker Mike Johnson sent it to Trump's desk on June 29. Trump had called it the 'most comprehensive housing legislation in American history' weeks before its passage, then abruptly reversed course, saying he'd sign it only if a voter ID bill also passed. The legislation is the first serious federal attempt in over 30 years to expand housing supply, including cutting red tape for developers, making it easier to finance housing, and banning large investors from buying single-family homes. Notably, it changes federal standards for manufactured housing, removing a requirement for steel chassis that currently limits the size and style of factory-built homes.

The U.S. has faced an acute housing shortage for years, driven by a combination of restrictive local zoning, high construction costs, and speculative buying by institutional investors. The shortage has pushed both home prices and rents to record levels in most major cities, making affordability one of the most politically potent issues heading into midterm elections.

Wildfire Kills at Least 12 in Southern Spain Amid European Heatwave

A wildfire in southern Spain has killed at least 12 people, with 19 still missing. Witnesses said the fire may have begun after a power line fell, though authorities have not confirmed this. The blaze is part of a pattern of sustained heatwaves scorching southern Europe — Spain recorded its highest daily average temperature since 1950 in June.

Southern Europe has become increasingly prone to destructive wildfires as climate change raises average temperatures and extends drought seasons. Spain, Greece, and Portugal have all suffered record-breaking fires in recent years, straining emergency services and raising questions about long-term land management policy.

SK Hynix Raises $26.5bn in Largest-Ever U.S. Listing by a Foreign Company

South Korean memory chipmaker SK Hynix priced its Nasdaq offering at $149 per American depositary receipt, raising $26.5 billion — the largest-ever U.S. listing by a non-American company, surpassing Alibaba's 2014 record. SK Hynix is the dominant supplier of high-bandwidth memory (HBM) chips — the specialized semiconductors that allow AI processors to access massive volumes of data at speed — and holds more than half the global HBM market, with Nvidia and Microsoft as primary customers. Its Seoul-listed shares are up over 600% in the past year, though they've fallen roughly 25% from their June peak, and hedge funds including Baillie Gifford and Coatue indicated interest in taking up to $7bn of the offering. The lead underwriters — Bank of America, Citi, Goldman Sachs, and JPMorgan — may share a fee pool running into nine figures, structured as 0.5% of funds raised plus a discretionary incentive fee.

Goldman Sachs and Wall Street Crackdown on Prediction Market Betting

Goldman Sachs has barred employees from wagering on essentially anything on prediction markets except sports and entertainment, with repeat violations potentially costing workers their jobs. The policy targets platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket, where employees with access to nonpublic information — from inflation forecasts to deal flow — could exploit an information edge. Company

SK Hynix

SK Hynix is a South Korean semiconductor company and the world's leading producer of high-bandwidth memory (HBM) chips — the specialized memory architecture that sits directly on AI processors to enable the extreme data speeds required by models like GPT and Gemini. It supplies more than half the global HBM market and counts Nvidia as its most critical customer, though Microsoft has also selected it for its proprietary AI chip program. The company's shares have risen over 600% in the past year on the Seoul exchange, and it recently overtook Samsung to become South Korea's largest company by market capitalization, joining the rare 'trillion-dollar club.' What makes its story remarkable: in 2001, SK Hynix was effectively insolvent and required a government-led bailout; it survived by making an early bet on HBM chips before there was significant market demand, a decision that is now paying off spectacularly as AI infrastructure spending accelerates. The chipmaking industry is notoriously cyclical, and analysts expect the current memory shortage to persist until at least 2028 — but a supply glut after that could reverse its fortunes just as sharply.
