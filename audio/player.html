U.S.-Iran Ceasefire Collapses; Hormuz Shipping Rattled

The fragile ceasefire between the U.S. and Iran broke down this week, with American forces striking more than 170 Iranian military targets over two days — including air defense systems, drone storage, and a railway bridge deep inside Iran. Trump declared the ceasefire "over" while simultaneously agreeing to continue negotiations, and Qatari mediators returned to Tehran in hopes of de-escalation. Traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil flows — dropped sharply as hostilities resumed, though Iran has been quietly rushing its own ships through. Iran's supreme leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, who was killed in the opening salvo of the conflict in February, was buried this week, and his son Mojtaba Khamenei is emerging as successor amid a power vacuum.

The U.S.-Iran war began earlier in 2026 following Khamenei's death. The Strait of Hormuz is a critical chokepoint for global oil exports; any disruption ripples immediately into fuel prices and airline costs. A ceasefire signed in June has now effectively collapsed.

Iran's Supreme Leader Absent After Khamenei Funeral; Succession Unclear

Ayatollah Mojtaba Khamenei, son and designated successor to the late supreme leader Ali Khamenei, did not appear at his father's funeral this week, fueling intense speculation about his health and grip on power. His absence has left an unprecedented vacuum at the top of Iran's government at the most critical moment of the war. Iran's parliament speaker insisted the conflict would "never end with Iran's surrender."

In Iran's political system, the supreme leader holds ultimate authority over the military, judiciary, and foreign policy — outranking the elected president. A succession struggle during active combat with the U.S. raises the risk of miscalculation or internal power conflicts.

Ukraine is waging a systematic campaign targeting Russian air defenses, supply lines, and energy infrastructure in Crimea, while separately making progress on the eastern front. Crimea is the peninsula Russia annexed from Ukraine in 2014 and has used as a key logistics hub for its invasion. NATO summits under Trump have become exercises in managing an unpredictable president while preserving the alliance's core commitments.

Mali's Jihadist Insurgency Intensifies, Threatening Bamako

JNIM, the Sahel's dominant jihadist group and an al-Qaeda affiliate, has intensified coordinated attacks across Mali since April, killing the country's defense minister and seizing key military strongholds. Since July, they have been striking towns both in Mali's north and near the capital, Bamako. A shipment of Russian weapons is reportedly en route to reinforce the junta.

Mali's military junta took power in a coup in 2020 and expelled French forces in favor of Russian support. JNIM's alliance with ethnic Tuareg separatists has created a more capable insurgency that is now testing the junta's hold on the country.

SK Hynix Makes History With $26.5B Nasdaq Debut — Largest-Ever U.S. Listing by a Foreign Company

South Korean memory-chip maker SK Hynix raised $26.5 billion by selling American depositary receipts (certificates representing foreign-listed shares) on the Nasdaq, pricing at $149 and opening above $170 — a 14% jump on day one. The listing breaks the record for the largest-ever U.S. IPO by a foreign company. SK Hynix is one of the world's three dominant memory chip producers alongside Samsung and Micron, and its chips are central to AI infrastructure — high-bandwidth memory used in Nvidia's AI accelerators is a key product. The strong debut signals that investor appetite for AI-adjacent semiconductor plays remains robust despite broader concerns about the sustainability of AI spending cycles.

Apollo Gazumps Castlelake to Buy easyJet for £5.7 Billion

Apollo Global Management swooped in with a last-minute £5.7 billion offer for easyJet, outbidding Castlelake — a U.S. private credit firm — which had already signed a £5.5 billion deal with the budget airline. The takeover returns easyJet to private ownership after more than two decades as a London-listed company. Apollo is bullish on easyJet's network of premium European airport slots and its large Airbus order book. The deal comes as the Iran war has sent jet fuel costs surging — Delta Air Lines reported a 77% year-on-year spike in fuel expenses this week — casting a shadow over the aviation sector just as it changes hands.

Apple Sues OpenAI for Trade Secret Theft

Apple filed a lawsuit against OpenAI and two of its developers, alleging they stole confidential information about unreleased Apple products and technology. Both named developers are former Apple employees who allegedly took proprietary files — one reportedly via a former work laptop — when they joined OpenAI. The suit widens a rift that began after the two companies signed a 2024 deal to integrate ChatGPT with Siri; OpenAI has denied the allegations. The lawsuit reflects a broader strategic tension: OpenAI is racing to develop its own consumer hardware devices so it can reach users directly rather than through Apple's ecosystem.

OpenAI and Google Are Selling Advanced AI Models to Pentagon-Blacklisted Chinese Tech Giants

OpenAI and Google have been selling access to their most advanced AI models to Chinese technology companies that are on the Pentagon's blacklist — entities the U.S. government has flagged as supporting China's military. Helsing: Europe's Most Valuable AI Defense Startup, Inside Its Secret Drone Factory

Helsing SE, the Munich-based AI defense startup founded in 2021 with backing from Spotify founder Daniel Ek, is raising roughly $1.2 billion at an ~$18 billion valuation — making it Europe's most valuable AI defense company. DealBook got exclusive access to its secret drone factory in southern Germany, where workers assemble the HX-2 attack drone: a 26-pound, AI-powered loitering munition (a drone that hovers over a battlefield and strikes on command) that costs as little as €17,500 and has been deployed in Ukraine since late 2024. The drones' AI software lets them continue operating even when GPS and communications are jammed by Russian electronic warfare. Germany's parliament this week approved a €220 million contract for Helsing to develop a broad suite of AI military systems, and the company is building its first unmanned fighter jet, the CA-1 Europa, targeting deployment in 2029.

The U.S. government has approved plans to test a large satellite mirror designed to reflect sunlight toward Earth, illuminating areas during nighttime hours. Company

Helsing SE

Helsing is a Munich-based AI defense startup founded in 2021 by three former McKinsey and defense ministry veterans, with early backing from Spotify founder Daniel Ek's VC firm. The company makes AI-powered attack drones and autonomous weapons systems designed to be produced cheaply and at scale — its HX-2 loitering munition (a kamikaze-style drone that hovers over a target, then strikes on command) costs as little as €17,500 versus more than $100 million for a conventional fighter jet. Thousands of HX-2s have been deployed in Ukraine since late 2024, giving Helsing a unique live-combat data flywheel: Ukrainian soldiers film missions, the company analyzes the footage, and the AI improves in near-real-time. Germany's parliament just approved a €220 million contract with Helsing, and the company is now raising roughly $1.2 billion at an ~$18 billion valuation. The surprising fact: Helsing's factory can be completely dismantled and relocated within a single day in case of a sabotage threat — and none of the other tenants in the industrial complex where it operates know it exists.
