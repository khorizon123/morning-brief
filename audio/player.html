US Strikes Iran After Iranian Navy Attacks Commercial Ship in Strait of Hormuz

The U.S. military launched strikes against Iran following an Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) attack on a commercial container ship transiting the Strait of Hormuz. Iran subsequently declared the Strait closed, and one crew member was reported missing. Iran has since been firing on U.S. allies in the Persian Gulf in retaliation for the overnight American strikes. The Iranian attack on the ship is expected to trigger further oil price volatility.

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman through which roughly 20% of the world's oil supply passes — it is one of the most strategically critical chokepoints on earth. Tensions between Iran and the West have been simmering for years over Iran's nuclear program and regional proxy activities; any disruption here sends immediate shockwaves through global energy markets.

Senator Lindsey Graham Dies at 71

Senator Lindsey Graham, Republican of South Carolina and a four-term Senate veteran, died Saturday evening of what his office described as a 'brief and sudden illness.' Graham had been one of President Trump's most stalwart Senate allies over the past decade. His death opens a Senate seat in a deep-red state, with South Carolina's Republican governor expected to appoint a successor.

Graham served in the Senate for more than two decades and was previously known as a centrist dealmaker before aligning closely with Trump. His death shifts the balance of Senate responsibilities and could affect ongoing legislative and confirmation priorities depending on the timing of an appointment.

Big Private Equity Firms Hoover Up Capital as Smaller Players Face 'Zombie' Risk

The largest private equity firms are pulling in a disproportionate share of new investor capital, widening the gap between the industry's haves and have-nots. The FT reports that a shrinking pool of fund recipients points to a coming wave of so-called 'zombie firms' — PE funds that can neither raise new money nor exit their existing investments, leaving them trapped managing deteriorating portfolios. This winner-take-all dynamic has intensified as institutional investors (pension funds, endowments, sovereign wealth funds) concentrate commitments with brand-name managers perceived as safer in an uncertain macro environment. Smaller and mid-market GPs (general partners, i.e., fund managers) face growing pressure to merge, specialize, or wind down. The trend has broader implications for the companies in those zombie portfolios, which may struggle to attract growth capital or strategic attention.

China Regulators Pressure Credit Agencies to Rein In Triple-A Corporate Bond Ratings

Chinese regulators are leaning on domestic credit rating agencies to limit the number of triple-A designations awarded to higher-interest corporate borrowers. Triple-A is the highest possible rating, theoretically reserved for only the most creditworthy issuers — but China's ratings industry has been widely criticized for ratings inflation, with a large share of bonds carrying top marks despite meaningful default risk. The crackdown is aimed at improving the reliability of ratings information and reducing moral hazard in the country's corporate bond market, which has suffered a string of high-profile defaults in recent years, particularly in real estate. More accurate ratings could raise borrowing costs for weaker issuers but would give investors better signals about underlying risk.

Russia Deploys Black-and-White Camouflaged Lorries to Confuse Drone AI in Ukraine

Drone warfare has become so dominant on the Ukrainian battlefield that both sides are now engineering countermeasures against the artificial intelligence systems guiding attacks. Russia has introduced peculiar black-and-white painted military lorries designed to confuse the machine-vision algorithms that drones use to identify and target vehicles. Machine vision — the AI technique that lets drones 'see' and classify objects — struggles when trained patterns don't match real-world appearances, and high-contrast geometric patterns can exploit those blind spots. The development represents a novel frontier in anti-AI tactics, essentially applying a modern twist on classic military camouflage logic. It also signals that the AI arms race has moved well beyond software into physical countermeasures on the ground.

Startups Pay White-Collar Professionals to Train AI Models to Do Their Jobs

A growing cottage industry has emerged in which AI startups pay lawyers, doctors, engineers, and other knowledge workers to teach AI models how to perform their professional tasks — essentially paying experts to encode and transfer their own expertise into systems designed to eventually replace them. The work typically involves annotating data, evaluating AI outputs for accuracy, and demonstrating complex reasoning step-by-step. The NYT describes the dynamic as 'bleak,' noting the uncomfortable irony of workers essentially accelerating their own automation. This practice, sometimes called 'AI training' or 'RLHF data collection' (reinforcement learning from human feedback), has become a significant line item in AI development budgets. It underscores a transitional labor market moment in which human expertise is temporarily in high demand — precisely to render it less necessary.

AI Rebrands Have Not Delivered Lasting Share Price Gains, FT Analysis Finds

Companies that pivoted their brand and narrative around artificial intelligence have mostly failed to sustain the valuation gains those announcements initially generated, according to an FT analysis. While AI pivots — where established firms reposition themselves as AI companies or announce major AI initiatives — often produce short-term stock pops, the boost tends to erode as investors look for evidence in actual earnings. The pattern echoes earlier 'blockchain' rebranding waves from the late 2010s, where similar enthusiasm faded once fundamentals reasserted themselves. The finding is a useful corrective to the idea that AI association alone is a durable value driver; markets appear increasingly skeptical of narrative without delivery.

UK Launches 'Innovators and Disrupters' Fellowship to Bring AI Expertise Into Government

The UK government has launched a fellowship program that embeds private-sector technologists — particularly AI and data specialists — inside public sector agencies on short-term 'high-impact tours of duty.' The goal is to accelerate the adoption of AI tools in government services, from benefits processing to healthcare administration. The scheme reflects a growing recognition that government IT lag is a structural problem, and that importing private-sector talent temporarily may be more tractable than large-scale civil service reskilling. The UK's National Health Service and HMRC (tax authority) are among the agencies reportedly benefiting from early placements. It parallels similar 'tech fellowship' experiments in the U.S. (such as the U.S. Digital Service) but is framed more explicitly around AI deployment.

Company

Airbnb

Airbnb operates the world's largest home-sharing marketplace, connecting property owners who rent out rooms or entire homes with travelers seeking alternatives to hotels. The company is in the spotlight right now because the 2026 FIFA World Cup — hosted across multiple U.S. cities — has given it a major marketing moment: data suggests price-conscious fans opted en masse for Airbnb listings over traditional hotels, which fell short of their own booking expectations despite heavy promotional campaigns. This real-world stress test underscores Airbnb's durability as a challenger to the hotel industry even for high-demand, nationally marketed events. One surprising fact: Airbnb was founded in 2008 when its founders literally rented out air mattresses in their San Francisco apartment to conference attendees — the origin of the 'air bed' in the name — and is now worth more than Marriott, Hilton, and Hyatt combined.
