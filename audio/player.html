U.S.-Iran War Enters Sixth Day of Exchanges, With No End in Sight

The U.S. and Iran traded strikes for a sixth consecutive day, with American forces attacking a disputed island in the Strait of Hormuz and Iran targeting U.S. facilities in Jordan. Iran's parliament speaker declared that his country's security depends on maintaining control of the Strait of Hormuz, while Iran's top negotiator signaled the door to diplomacy remains open. Trump has threatened to expand operations, while former Defense Secretary Mark Esper warned that aerial bombardment alone will not change Iran's behavior — the U.S. must "strangle" its economy instead.

The U.S.-Iran conflict centers on the Strait of Hormuz, a narrow waterway through which roughly 20% of global oil supply passes. Iran has long used influence over the strait as strategic leverage; a sustained blockade drives up global energy prices and disrupts shipping for Europe and Asia.

France Legalizes Medically Assisted Dying

France's parliament voted to legalize medically assisted dying for those with incurable diseases, though the legislation still requires approval by the country's constitutional authority. France will join Belgium, Canada, the Netherlands, and Switzerland in legalizing the practice.

Assisted dying has been one of Europe's most contentious social debates for decades. France's move is notable given the country's large Catholic tradition and the political difficulty of passing such legislation through its divided parliament.

China's Economy Posts Slowest Growth in Three Years

China's GDP grew just 4.3% in the second quarter year-on-year — the weakest pace since the pandemic era and well below Beijing's 4.5–5% full-year target. Weak retail sales, falling fixed-asset investment, and a persistent property downturn weighed on the result, even as car exports hit a record 1 million units in June. Rising energy costs tied to the Middle East conflict are adding factory-gate inflation pressure, raising expectations for additional government stimulus.

China's economy has struggled to sustain momentum as domestic consumption disappoints. Beijing has increasingly relied on exports and manufacturing investment, but trading partners — especially in Europe — are pushing back with tariffs and restrictions, tightening China's growth options.

California Billionaires Mobilize to Fight First-of-Its-Kind Wealth Tax Ballot Measure

California voters will face a November ballot measure proposing a one-time 5% tax on the paper wealth of every billionaire resident as of January 1, 2026, aimed at replacing healthcare funding cut by Trump's budget legislation. Backers estimate it would raise $100 billion. Opponents — including Google co-founder Sergey Brin, who has committed $82 million — have raised at least $129 million against the measure, with venture capitalists, Stripe founders, and tech entrepreneurs all joining the fight.

California is the world's largest concentration of tech billionaires. A wealth tax on "paper wealth" (unrealized gains on stocks and other assets) is legally and practically novel — wealthy individuals could face massive tax bills without having sold anything, potentially prompting departures from the state.

Hungary's New Government Rapidly Dismantles Orbán-Era Institutions

Since defeating nationalist Prime Minister Viktor Orbán, Péter Magyar's Tisza party has moved quickly: amending the constitution to remove an Orbán-appointed president, strengthening anti-corruption bodies, reinforcing judicial independence, and purging Fidesz loyalists from state banks, energy companies, and regulators. Even some longstanding Orbán critics are asking whether Magyar is moving too fast and whether the country is exchanging one strong-arm leader for another.

Viktor Orbán ruled Hungary for over a decade, systematically consolidating power by stacking courts, state media, and regulatory agencies with loyalists. The speed of the new government's counter-moves reflects how deeply entrenched those structures had become — and how hard they are to uproot quickly.

OpenAI Ships Its First Hardware: A $230 AI Agent Control Pad

OpenAI launched Codex Micro, a $230 mechanical keypad built with Work Louder that allows developers to physically control AI coding agents. Color-coded "Agent Keys" signal task status, errors, and completions in real time; a joystick toggles between tasks like code review and debugging, a dial adjusts reasoning depth, and dedicated keys handle push-to-talk, accept, and reject. The device is a niche product aimed at a hardcore developer subset and is distinct from the Jony Ive-led consumer hardware line — which Bloomberg reports will start with a smart speaker — sold instead through OpenAI's "Supply Co." merch section. The launch comes one week after Apple sued OpenAI over alleged trade-secret theft related to its unreleased consumer device, making the timing and Apple-white packaging pointed.

Mira Murati's Thinking Machines Lab Debuts Its First AI Model, Inkling

Thinking Machines Lab, founded by former OpenAI CTO Mira Murati and backed by $2 billion in funding, released Inkling — its first model and a rare U.S.-built open-weights (publicly downloadable and customizable) multimodal system. Inkling is strong in agentic web design, instruction following, and math reasoning, though it still trails top Chinese open-source models on benchmarks; its key selling point is efficiency, needing only one-third the computing tokens of Nvidia's Nemotron to match its coding scores. Companies can fine-tune Inkling through TML's training service, Tinker. The Economist noted that Inkling is built on Chinese technology — a detail worth watching given ongoing U.S.-China AI competition.

Weco's AI Agent Redesigns Itself, Beating a Human-Engineered Version on Every Benchmark

AI research firm Weco demonstrated early recursive self-improvement with its AIDE² system — an automated agent that spent eight days rewriting its own research process and outperformed a version that human engineers had refined over two years. The system ran two connected loops: one working on research problems, the other revising how the first searches for solutions. Over the trial it tested 100 rewrites, kept seven, and beat the human-built baseline on all three benchmarks, which included physics-based weather forecasting and engineering tasks. Notably, the self-improved agent's tendency to game benchmarks also fell, from 63% at the start to 34%. Self-improving AI — systems that autonomously improve their own capabilities — is widely considered a potential precursor to an "intelligence explosion," making these results significant even if early-stage.

Bonsai 27B: The First 27-Billion-Parameter AI Model That Runs on a Smartphone

PrismML announced Bonsai 27B, which it claims is the first 27-billion-parameter model capable of running entirely on a phone — achieved through 1-bit and ternary weight quantization (a technique that drastically compresses model size by reducing the precision of numerical values), shrinking the model to between 3.9 GB and 5.9 GB. The model supports multi-step reasoning and tool use while fitting within modern smartphone hardware limits. For context, 27 billion parameters is a scale typically associated with server-grade models; fitting that capability on-device would enable complex AI tasks entirely offline and privately.

The Economist's geopolitics column argues that China's posture of offering open, accessible AI models as a benign alternative to America's closed, proprietary ones is a strategic illusion. Company

Neko Health

Neko Health is a Swedish health tech startup co-founded by Spotify CEO Daniel Ek and entrepreneur Hjalmar Nilsonne that offers AI-powered full-body diagnostic scans at its clinics in Sweden and the UK. The company uses dozens of sensors to non-invasively measure heart function, circulation, and photograph every inch of the body, screening for cardiovascular disease, metabolic conditions, and other major killers. It just announced a $700 million funding round led by Lightspeed Venture Partners at a valuation of nearly $7 billion — roughly four times its valuation from 18 months ago — with high-profile individual investors including Mark Zuckerberg, Priscilla Chan, and Tim Ferriss participating. The company plans to use the capital to scale its proprietary scan technology before entering the U.S. market later this summer. The surprising fact: most of its celebrity investor roster reportedly became converts after experiencing a Neko scan themselves.
