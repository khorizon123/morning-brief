U.S.-Iran Ceasefire Collapses Into Tit-for-Tat Strikes

President Trump declared the eight-week U.S.-Iran ceasefire "over" after Iran attacked three commercial ships in the Strait of Hormuz and struck U.S. military bases in Bahrain and Kuwait. The U.S. military responded with strikes on roughly 90 targets in southern Iran, framing the campaign as retaliation for "unjustified aggression against commercial shipping." Ship traffic through the Strait has ground to near-standstill, though Brent crude — while elevated — remains well below its wartime peak as regional energy infrastructure was not struck. Trump simultaneously revoked a sanctions waiver that had allowed Iran to export oil, and at NATO's Ankara summit signaled he is weighing options ranging from an all-out blockade to an extended low-intensity conflict in the Gulf.

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman through which roughly a fifth of global oil trade passes. Iran uses control of the strait as geopolitical leverage; closing it drives up energy prices worldwide. Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, Iran's long-ruling supreme leader, was killed at the outbreak of the conflict in February, and the country is now dominated by hardline military figures who may welcome continued escalation.

IMF Cuts Global Growth Forecast, Warns of Fresh Inflation from Renewed Gulf Conflict

The IMF lowered its 2026 global growth forecast to 3%, down from 3.5% in 2025, and raised its inflation projection to 4.7% for the year — higher than its April estimate — citing wartime commodity pressures and supply-chain disruptions. The Fund warned that oil inventory buffers are approaching "stress levels" should Hormuz disruptions persist. Markets reacted quickly: a Fed rate hike is now priced in by October, a European Central Bank hike by September, and a Bank of England move fully expected by year-end. Separately, the IMF named former Bank of England policymaker Silvana Tenreyro as its next chief economist.

The IMF publishes its World Economic Outlook periodically to assess global macroeconomic conditions. Rate hike expectations matter because higher interest rates increase borrowing costs for governments, businesses, and homeowners worldwide — compounding the economic pain already caused by elevated energy prices.

Russia Bans Diesel Exports After Ukrainian Drone Strikes on Refineries, Sending Futures Up 11%

U.S. diesel futures surged 11% on Wednesday — their biggest single-day move since 2022 — after Russia temporarily banned diesel exports following Ukrainian drone attacks on its refineries. Russia was the world's second-largest seaborne diesel exporter before the ban, supplying roughly 11% of global supply. The disruption compounds already-tight diesel markets stressed by the Hormuz closure and wartime demand. Aldo Spanjer of BNP Paribas noted that "even relatively small changes to balances have a pretty strong impact on prices" in an already tight market.

Diesel is the workhorse fuel of global logistics — trucks, trains, ships, and farm equipment all depend on it. A spike in diesel prices flows quickly through to the cost of virtually every physical good. Ukraine's drone campaign against Russian energy infrastructure has been an ongoing feature of the war and has now disrupted Russian fuel exports for the first time at this scale.

NATO Summit Closes With Trump Backing Ukraine Patriot Production and Striking Allies

At NATO's summit in Ankara, Turkey, Trump agreed to grant Ukraine a license to manufacture Patriot air-defense interceptors — a long-sought Kyiv demand — while also endorsing deeper Ukrainian strikes inside Russian territory. The concessions marked a significant shift from months of friction, though Trump also spent the summit insulting several allies (calling Spaniards "hopeless, bad people") and claiming he would consider seizing Iran's Kharg Island oil terminal. NATO reaffirmed support for Ukraine and announced new European defense spending commitments; the final read was cautiously positive for the alliance's cohesion.

The Patriot is an advanced surface-to-air missile system that Kyiv uses to defend against Russian ballistic missiles. Ukraine has long pushed for permission to produce its own interceptors rather than relying entirely on Western stockpiles, which are limited. Granting a manufacturing license is symbolically significant even though actual production capacity would take years to build.

Sudan's RSF Accused of Genocide as UN Says 59,000 Dead

The United Nations said actions by Sudan's Rapid Support Forces bear the "markers of genocide," following a UN-led investigation that found evidence of mass killings and forced starvation. The RSF is a paramilitary group fighting the Sudanese army in a conflict now two years old. At least 59,000 people have been killed, and the RSF has previously denied such accusations.

Sudan has been engulfed in civil war since April 2023, when fighting broke out between the national army and the RSF, a powerful paramilitary that grew out of the Janjaweed militias. The conflict has triggered one of the world's worst humanitarian crises, with millions displaced. A formal genocide designation from the UN would increase pressure for international intervention or sanctions.

Oil Gyrates as Hormuz Crisis Deepens; Treasury Yields Rise, Stocks Hold

Markets are sending conflicting signals amid the renewed Gulf conflict. Brent crude briefly topped $80/barrel before easing to roughly $78, up about 5% on the week — well below its wartime peak of $120 but trending higher on renewed fighting. The CBOE VIX (Wall Street's fear gauge) ticked up, yet S&P 500 futures remained in the green, reflecting a market that has largely priced in low-level Gulf instability. The 10-year Treasury yield, which underpins mortgages and commercial loans, hit 4.589% — up more than half a percentage point since U.S. strikes on Iran began — as bondholders price in persistent inflation risk. Gold sits above $4,000/oz and copper rose nearly 2% on the day.

Intel Makes Comeback While Nvidia Loses Its AI Crown

Nvidia — long the engine of the AI trade — shed nearly $1 trillion in market cap over two months and now trades at 18x forward earnings, cheaper than both the S&P 500 (20x) and Nasdaq 100 (23x). Despite still earning $58.3 billion in its most recent quarter with a ~$5 trillion market cap, its multiple compression signals investors see the AI infrastructure buildout peak behind them for the chipmaker. Intel is emerging as the surprise beneficiary: after handing the U.S. government a 10% stake last summer amid a near-death spiral, it has drastically improved manufacturing, and Trump recently announced a collaboration with Apple on U.S. chip production. HSBC analyst Frank Lee doubled his Intel price target to $200. Meanwhile, SK Hynix's U.S. IPO — expected to be Wall Street's second-biggest listing after SpaceX's recent $86bn offering — attracted fierce demand.

Apple Signs $30bn Broadcom Chip Deal; AI Spending Fuels Airfreight Surge

Apple signed a $30 billion, multi-year agreement with Broadcom to produce more than 15 billion semiconductors in the U.S., including at Broadcom's Colorado facility — part of CEO Tim Cook's pledged $600 billion U.S. investment commitment to the Trump administration. The deal is one of the largest private domestic manufacturing commitments of the AI era. Separately, the AI infrastructure buildout is reshaping global freight: air-cargo volumes from Asia to North America rose nearly 20% year-over-year in May as companies air-freight server racks and semiconductors rather than wait for ocean shipping. Spot airfreight rates on the Asia-North America corridor jumped 36% year-over-year in June, with demand displacing cheap e-commerce packages that previously dominated cargo holds.

SpaceX AI and Cursor Release Grok 4.5, Their First Joint Model

SpaceX AI (xAI) and Cursor — the AI-powered code editor that xAI acquired for $60 billion — jointly launched Grok 4.5, their first co-developed model. It runs at 80 tokens per second (rivaling much smaller "flash" models) while claiming a 4x efficiency gain over Anthropic's Opus 4.8. Pricing is aggressive: $2/$6 per million input/output tokens versus $5/$25 for Opus 4.8, and it's temporarily free inside Cursor. Benchmarks put it in similar territory to Opus 4.8 and GPT-5.5 on coding, agentic, and knowledge tasks. Elon Musk teased an even larger successor model coming next month. The release signals the acquisition is already bearing fruit in both directions — Cursor's coding models are also improving.

OpenAI Launches GPT-Live, a Full-Duplex Voice Model That Can Listen and Reason Simultaneously

OpenAI rolled out GPT-Live, a next-generation voice model built on a "full-duplex" architecture — meaning it processes audio input continuously rather than waiting for a pause before responding, removing the awkward turn-based feel of earlier voice AI. When a question requires deep reasoning, Live can silently delegate to a stronger backend model before answering, and can surface visual cards alongside spoken responses. OpenAI's internal tests found users preferred GPT-Live over the old Advanced Voice Mode in roughly 3 of 4 head-to-head comparisons. The company is also releasing GPT-5.6 in three variants (Sol, Terra, and Luna) spanning flagship, balanced, and low-cost tiers.

Microsoft has started substituting its own in-house AI models for OpenAI's and Anthropic's in consumer products like Excel and Outlook, a move that reflects both cost pressure and strategic necessity. The shift also shows Microsoft's own model capabilities have matured enough to be competitive in everyday-use scenarios, even if not at the frontier.

DeepSeek Is Building Its Own Chips to Reduce Reliance on Nvidia and Huawei

China's DeepSeek — the lab that rattled markets earlier this year with its efficient open-source models — is now moving into hardware, meeting with chip and hardware partners and hiring engineers to design data center inference chips. The move is a direct response to U.S. export controls that restrict DeepSeek's access to Nvidia's highest-end GPUs. Building proprietary silicon would reduce its dependence on both Nvidia and Huawei's Ascend chips, giving it more control in a market where compute access is expected to remain constrained. The timeline is unclear, as chip design and manufacturing at scale typically takes years.

Power Transformers Are Choking the AI Build-Out

A surprising bottleneck has emerged in the AI infrastructure race: century-old electrical transformer technology. Transformers — the large devices that step electricity up or down in voltage, not the neural network kind — are facing severe supply shortages as demand from AI data centers spikes. About 90% of all electricity passes through one on its path from generation to consumption, and the most powerful industrial variants face multi-year order backlogs, driving up prices and threatening to delay data center construction timelines. The bottleneck sits entirely outside the usual AI conversation about chips and compute.

Company

SK Hynix

SK Hynix is South Korea's second-largest semiconductor company and the world's leading maker of high-bandwidth memory (HBM) chips — the specialized memory that sits directly alongside AI accelerators like Nvidia's H100 and H200 GPUs and is essential to their performance. It sits at the center of the current AI infrastructure boom, with HBM demand vastly outstripping supply. The company's U.S. IPO, expected to price imminently, is drawing extraordinary demand and is set to be the second-largest Wall Street listing of the year, behind only SpaceX. One surprising fact: despite being a crown jewel of the AI era, South Korea's broader stock market recently fell into bear territory (down 20%+ from its June peak) as investors worry the AI chip cycle may be turning — illustrating how even sector leaders can't immunize a broader index.
