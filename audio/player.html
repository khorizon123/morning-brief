Trump Declares U.S.-Iran Ceasefire 'Over' at NATO Summit

President Trump said at the NATO summit in Ankara, 'To me, I think it's over,' referring to the preliminary ceasefire reached last month with Iran. The statement followed a night of exchanges: the U.S. military struck 80 Iranian targets after Iran attacked three tankers in the Strait of Hormuz, and Iran's Revolutionary Guard responded with missile and drone strikes on U.S. facilities in Bahrain and Kuwait. Washington also revoked a waiver allowing Iran to sell oil on the open market — a key concession in the original truce — and Trump blasted NATO allies for refusing to back the U.S. campaign against Iran.

The U.S. and Iran signed a preliminary ceasefire roughly a month ago intended to reopen the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow passage through which a large share of the world's seaborne oil flows — and create space for longer peace talks. Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, Iran's longtime supreme leader, died during the conflict; his funeral is drawing millions of mourners across the region.

Oil Spikes, Markets Slide as Iran Tensions Reignite Inflation Fears

Brent crude surged past $78 a barrel — up more than 8% since Monday — on the breakdown of the ceasefire. S&P 500 futures fell, global stocks dropped, and the 10-year U.S. Treasury yield climbed to 4.58%, a multi-week high, as investors priced in renewed inflation risk from higher energy costs. Markets are now fully pricing in a Fed rate hike by October, and a Bank of England hike by year-end — both earlier than previously expected. The New York Fed's latest consumer sentiment survey showed inflation expectations at their highest since September 2023.

Higher oil prices feed directly into inflation because energy is an input for almost everything — transportation, manufacturing, food production. When oil spikes, central banks face a difficult choice: raise rates to fight inflation (risking a slowdown) or hold steady to protect growth (risking prices spiraling).

Marine Le Pen Declares Presidential Run After Court Lifts Office Ban

French far-right leader Marine Le Pen announced she will run for president in next year's election after an appeals court shortened her ban from seeking public office — though it upheld her embezzlement conviction. Le Pen, who would be making her fourth presidential bid, enters the race as the front-runner to succeed Emmanuel Macron. Her protégé Jordan Bardella, who had been expected to lead the party's campaign if she was barred, will serve as her proposed prime minister instead.

Le Pen leads the Rassemblement National (National Rally), France's dominant far-right party. French presidential elections use a two-round system: if no candidate wins an outright majority in the first round, the top two face off — and polls have long shown Le Pen as a strong contender to reach that runoff.

Japan's Borrowing Costs Hit 30-Year High as Debt Fears Mount

Japanese government bond yields have surged to their highest level since 1996, driven by investor concerns about Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's $2 trillion long-term spending plan and a weakening yen. The sell-off in long-term debt reflects growing anxiety that Japan's already-massive debt burden will become unmanageable, with the dollar-yen rate now above 162.

Japan has the world's highest public debt-to-GDP ratio, historically managed because rates were kept near zero for decades. When yields rise, borrowing becomes more expensive for the government, potentially forcing spending cuts or tax increases — a significant policy shift for a country that has run large deficits for years.

Blue Origin Raises $10 Billion at $130 Billion Valuation in First-Ever Outside Funding Round

Jeff Bezos' rocket company Blue Origin is closing in on a $10 billion fundraise — its first time accepting outside investment in its 25-year history — at a pre-money valuation of $130 billion. Coatue Management is set to lead with a $4 billion commitment, Bezos himself will contribute an additional $2 billion, and the remaining $4 billion is expected from large institutional investors. The round gives Blue Origin a fixed valuation useful for future financing and could relieve Bezos of the need to keep selling Amazon stock to fund the company. Investors appear undeterred by the explosion of a New Glenn rocket during a test in May, focusing instead on Blue Origin's future projects including TeraWave, a high-capacity satellite communications network targeting data centers and other high-priority customers — analogous to SpaceX's Starlink.

UniCredit Secures ~50% Stake in Commerzbank in $47.8 Billion Takeover Bid

Italy's UniCredit has increased its holding in Commerzbank to close to 50%, bringing CEO Andrea Orcel within striking distance of taking full control of Germany's second-largest lender. The bid values Commerzbank at about $47.8 billion. UniCredit still needs formal clearance from the European Central Bank to exceed 30% — a regulatory threshold that triggers a mandatory full bid offer. Commerzbank's management and Germany's government, which holds a 12% stake, have been resisting the takeover. UniCredit's ultimate aim is to combine Commerzbank with HypoVereinsbank, another German lender it already owns.

Private Equity Faces a Nine-Year Backlog of Unsold Companies

Private equity firms are sitting on 13,500 unsold U.S. portfolio companies as of June 30, nearly triple the count from 2006 — and at the current pace, it would take roughly nine years to clear the backlog. The problem is particularly acute in software, where the 'SaaS-Pocalypse' — a collapse in software valuations driven by AI disruption fears — has hammered companies bought at peak 2020-21 valuations with heavy debt loads. Not all bad news: the industry managed 16 IPOs in the first half of the year, the most since 2021.

SK Hynix's $28 Billion U.S. Listing Is Reportedly Several Times Oversubscribed

South Korean memory chipmaker SK Hynix is seeking to raise approximately $28 billion via a Nasdaq listing this Friday — which would be the largest-ever initial listing by a foreign company in America. Despite the boom, its forward price-to-earnings ratio sits around 7x — low because memory is a notoriously cyclical industry, and investors are already discounting the next inevitable downturn.

Apple Commits $30 Billion to Broadcom Chips, Largest Pledge Under Trump-Era U.S. Investment Plan

Apple has pledged to spend more than $30 billion on Broadcom chips over five years, the largest individual commitment so far under its broader $600 billion U.S. investment pledge made to the Trump administration. The deal secures continued development of custom ASIC (application-specific integrated circuit) chips — specialized processors that handle AI-related tasks more efficiently than general-purpose chips — across multiple generations of Apple products, with advanced AI server deployments targeted as early as 2027. The partnership also enables Broadcom to expand a chip-manufacturing facility in Colorado.

Beijing Weighs Restricting Chinese AI Models' Use Outside China

Chinese government officials have reportedly met with ByteDance, Alibaba, and Zhipu AI (maker of the GLM series) to discuss rules that would limit overseas access to China's most advanced AI models — mirroring the U.S. export controls on frontier AI models introduced earlier this year. Discussions cover both closed and open-source models, startup funding limits, and penalties for leaking proprietary AI. The move would make the risk of losing AI model access two-sided: Western users could find popular, low-cost Chinese models cut off just as American labs face U.S. government scrutiny. Both nations are increasingly treating frontier AI as a national security asset.

Anthropic Research Reveals 'J-Space': Internal Neural Patterns in Claude That May Underpin Reasoning

Anthropic published a research paper introducing the concept of 'J-space' — internal neural activation patterns in Claude that emerge during training without being explicitly designed. Researchers believe these patterns allow Claude to internally reason through multi-step problems and modulate its own thinking, distinguishing deliberate processing from more automatic responses. The finding has potential implications for AI safety, as it may provide a window into monitoring internal model behavior for signs of misbehavior, and raises philosophical questions about the nature of AI cognition.
